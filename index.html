About the Project

A transit-focused redesign for movement, access, and everyday use.

This project reimagines Montefiore Square in Harlem as a more effective public space for people moving between the subway, buses, school, and nearby neighborhood destinations. Instead of treating the plaza as leftover space, this proposal treats it as an important part of everyday public life and neighborhood movement.

Located along Broadway near the 137th Street–City College station, the plaza functions as a transfer space between transit, sidewalks, and neighborhood destinations. The goal is to transform it from a confusing pass-through area into a clear multimodal hub that supports walking, waiting, orientation, and safer connections between transit points.

The Problem

Why the current plaza does not work well as a transit space.

Existing Issues

Congestion builds near the main pedestrian entry points because large numbers of people leave the subway and move toward Broadway, bus stops, and City College at the same time. The plaza currently does little to organize these overlapping flows.

Circulation between transit stops is unclear. Instead of obvious routes, pedestrians are forced to improvise their own paths, which creates conflict between people passing through quickly and people waiting in place.

Wayfinding is weak for first-time users. Riders transferring between subway and bus often have to stop, look around, or check their phones because there are few clear directional cues in the space itself.

Essential Question

How can Montefiore Square be redesigned to better serve people moving between transit, school, shopping, and neighborhood destinations?

Project Goal

The goal is to reduce congestion, improve navigation, and create a safer, more comfortable transit space for everyday users. A successful redesign should make it easier to understand where to walk, where to wait, and how to move through the plaza without confusion or conflict.

Why It Matters

Unsafe and confusing crossings make the site harder to use for students, older adults, bus riders, and first-time visitors. Because pedestrians approach the site from Broadway, West 138th Street, and Hamilton Place, the design must organize multiple directions of movement at once.

Site and Context

Understanding Montefiore Square as a movement space.

Existing site conditions showing congestion, unclear pedestrian routes, and a street environment that prioritizes movement through the area without clearly supporting pedestrian comfort or orientation.

Movement Analysis

Montefiore Square sits in a location where pedestrians are constantly transitioning between the subway, nearby bus stops, sidewalks, and neighborhood destinations. Because of that, the site feels busy but not well organized. People are often forced to improvise their routes instead of being guided clearly through the plaza.

Most pedestrians exit the subway and move northeast toward City College or continue along Broadway. That creates overlapping circulation patterns near entrances, crossings, and waiting areas. Instead of supporting smooth movement, the current layout increases confusion and makes the space harder to read for both daily users and first-time visitors.

Waiting and Use Patterns

A transit plaza must do more than move people through. It also needs to support waiting, pausing, and decision-making. Right now, the site offers very few clearly defined places where someone can sit, orient themselves, or wait comfortably for a bus.

This matters because transit users do not all move at the same speed or use the space in the same way. Students, older adults, bus riders, and neighborhood pedestrians all need a plaza that separates fast circulation from more stationary forms of use.

Peak Hour Conditions

During the morning commute, the plaza acts as a diffusion zone as riders leave the subway and spread toward City College, Broadway, and surrounding blocks. During the afternoon and after-school period, those flows reverse as students and commuters move back toward transit.

Midday conditions are less intense, but movement is still active because the plaza serves students between classes, neighborhood residents, and people making errands or transit transfers. This means the plaza must work at both peak and off-peak times, not just rush hour.

Current Problems

Subway riders, bus riders, and neighborhood pedestrians currently share the same space without strong guidance. This creates conflict where people want to move quickly through the plaza while others need to stop and wait.

The busiest conflict points are likely at the subway exit, the bus stop edge, and the crossings that connect Broadway to the open plaza space.

Proposed Logic

The redesigned plaza separates fast circulation from waiting zones. Main pedestrian routes are kept clear and legible, while seating, shade, and rest areas are located beside — not inside — those movement paths.

Wayfinding should appear exactly where decisions happen: at subway exits, bus stop edges, and points where riders choose between Broadway, City College, and nearby neighborhood destinations.

Design Options

Testing multiple directions before choosing a final proposal.

Option 1: Green Shelter

Option 1 focuses on comfort by introducing shade, greenery, and more seating. This approach makes the plaza feel calmer, softer, and more welcoming for people who want to sit, wait, or spend time there.

Its greatest strength is atmosphere. It improves the experience of staying in the plaza and gives the site a stronger public-space identity. However, it does not fully solve the circulation and navigation issues that affect daily transit users.

Option 2: Wayfinding Plaza

Option 2 focuses on movement by creating clearer pedestrian paths and stronger wayfinding between subway exits, bus stops, and sidewalks.

Its greatest strength is circulation. It creates a clearer logic for movement, but it needs comfort elements to feel complete as a public space.

Comparison and Design Decision

The final proposal combines the strongest parts of both options. Option 1 improves comfort, shade, and atmosphere, while Option 2 improves circulation, navigation, and transfer clarity. Instead of choosing one over the other, the final scheme treats both movement and comfort as necessary parts of a successful transit plaza.

Final Proposal

A balanced transit plaza for movement, comfort, and everyday use.

Final concept combining clear routes, shaded seating, and stronger wayfinding into one integrated transit plaza.

Key Design Moves

  • Painted pavement arrows directing riders to subway exits and bus stops
  • Pole-mounted wayfinding signs at major decision points
  • Tactile paving and clearer crossings for accessibility
  • Shaded waiting zones with benches and ADA-friendly seating
  • Green planting edges that organize space without blocking circulation
  • Potential future digital kiosks with real-time transit information

Waiting Zone Strategy

The waiting zone is designed for multiple user groups. Bus riders need shelter, seating, and sightlines toward arriving buses. Students may only need a short-term place to pause or meet a friend. Older adults need seating with back support, armrests, and shade.

For that reason, the proposal separates waiting from the fastest movement routes. Seating is placed along the edges of circulation, not in the middle of it, so the plaza can support both efficiency and comfort at the same time.

The final design combines clear pedestrian paths with comfortable seating areas, creating a space that supports both movement and waiting. It improves circulation without making the plaza feel harsh or purely functional.

Implementation and Budget

Keeping the proposal realistic through phased improvements.

Phase 1

Painted wayfinding graphics, temporary seating, and transit connection signage. This phase is the most affordable and could function as a quick-build test of the proposal.

Concept range: $15,000–$30,000

Phase 2

Durable seating, shaded waiting zones, improved paving, planted edges, and permanent wayfinding signage. This phase makes the transit hub more comfortable and legible.

Concept range: $95,000–$160,000

Phase 3

Raised crossings, integrated lighting, digital transit displays, and a permanent transit identity for the plaza. This phase represents the full build-out.

Concept range: $150,000–$250,000

Funding and Partnerships

Potential implementation partners include NYC DOT, the NYC Plaza Program, the MTA, and City College. Because Montefiore Square was already improved through a city capital project, a future phase could build on that public investment rather than starting from zero.

Community Context

Who this redesign is for.

The plaza is used by a diverse mix of City College students, local residents, bus riders, commuters, and older adults. Because of that, a successful design cannot focus on only one type of user. It has to support both fast movement and slower, more accessible use.

Bilingual wayfinding and accessible seating are especially important in a neighborhood where transit is central to daily life. Designing for the most vulnerable users makes the plaza better for everyone.

Reflection on AI

What AI helped with and what still required human judgment.

AI helped generate visual ideas quickly and made it easier to compare different plaza concepts, especially for shade, greenery, and wayfinding. It was useful for testing how different design strategies might look without building full drawings from scratch.

However, some AI-generated results were too generic and did not always match the texture and density of upper Manhattan. Several images looked more like idealized pedestrian spaces than realistic transit infrastructure, so they had to be judged critically rather than accepted as-is.

Site observation could not be replaced by AI. Only direct observation can show where people cut diagonally across the plaza, where they stop to check directions, and where movement conflicts happen between crossings, subway exits, and bus stops.

Conclusion

This redesign improves safety, movement, and usability by treating Montefiore Square as a true transit hub rather than a leftover plaza. By combining wayfinding, waiting space, accessibility, and phased implementation, the project creates a stronger and more realistic vision for the site.
